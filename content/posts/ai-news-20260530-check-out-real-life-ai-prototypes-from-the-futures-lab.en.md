  - q: "How can AI builders apply the Waterloo Futures Lab approach to their own products?"
    a: "Start by identifying a specific user group that traditional tools cannot serve well, then map the concrete limitations they face daily. Pinpoint the smallest viable AI intervention that removes a real barrier, not a hypothetical one. Build a prototype focused on that single friction point, ship it to actual users, and iterate based on observed behavior. Skip the temptation to start from model capabilities and search for use cases. The Waterloo students chose sign language learners because the barrier was concrete and underserved, not because the technology was impressive."
  - q: "What are the limits and risks of AI sign language tutors?"
    a: "AI sign language tutors face accuracy gaps when recognizing regional sign variations, handling subtle facial expressions, or interpreting grammatical structures unique to signed languages like ASL. Camera quality, lighting, and signing speed all degrade recognition reliability. These prototypes work best as practice companions, not replacements for Deaf instructors or community immersion. Privacy is another concern since video-based AI requires processing biometric movement data. Builders should be transparent about accuracy rates, never market these tools as fluency guarantees, and design them to complement human instruction rather than substitute for it."
  - q: "Why does needs-first AI design outperform capability-first design?"
    a: "Needs-first design starts with a documented user problem and asks whether AI can solve it efficiently. Capability-first design starts with a model feature and hunts for applications, often producing solutions nobody asked for. Needs-first products achieve product-market fit faster because the problem is pre-validated. They also face lower competition since underserved niches attract fewer copycats. Capability-first products tend to crowd into saturated categories like chatbots and image generators. The Waterloo Futures Lab succeeds because students interview real users first, then constrain the AI to solve that specific barrier."

  - q: "What common mistakes do AI builders make when copying academic prototypes?"
    a: "The biggest mistake is copying the surface feature without copying the discovery process. Builders see a sign language tutor demo and rush to clone it, skipping the user interviews that made the original valuable. Another error is scaling prematurely: academic prototypes work in controlled conditions with motivated users, but consumer launches face messy real-world variance the prototype never tested. Builders also underestimate the domain expertise required, assuming a working demo means the problem is solved. Always replicate the methodology first, validate with your own user base, and budget for the messy operational work academia skips."
  - q: "How does the Waterloo Futures Lab compare to corporate AI research labs?"

Academic labs like Waterloo Futures Lab optimize for educational outcomes and applied prototypes, freeing students to chase niche problems without revenue pressure. Corporate labs ship infrastructure; academic labs ship proof-of-concept applications. For builders, academic labs offer a richer source of underserved-market signals, while corporate labs reveal where foundational capabilities are heading. Watch both: corporate research for what becomes possible, academic research for what becomes useful."
