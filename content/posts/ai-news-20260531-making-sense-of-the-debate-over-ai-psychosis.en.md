  - q: "What is AI psychosis and why are tech CEOs at higher risk?"
    a: "AI psychosis describes a pattern where decision-makers develop near-faith-based trust in AI outputs, treating statistical predictions as objective truth. CEOs face elevated risk because three structural factors stack up: their position creates information echo chambers where dissent is filtered out, their compensation and reputation depend on AI-driven business gains, and constant external hype around AI capabilities reinforces overconfidence. The result is judgment distortion that seeps from one person into the entire organization's decision framework, turning a personal cognitive bias into a company-wide blind spot that shapes hiring, product strategy, and capital allocation."
  - q: "How can I tell if I'm over-relying on AI for decisions?"
    a: "Run a reverse-question test after any AI-assisted decision. Ask yourself: if the model had given the opposite answer, how would I respond? If you cannot articulate a counter-case, or you feel resistance to even considering one, you have crossed from reference signal into faith-based reliance. Other warning signs include skipping verification steps you used to perform, dismissing human expert input that contradicts AI output, and explaining away cases where AI was wrong as edge cases. Healthy AI use treats every output as a hypothesis requiring confirmation, not a verdict."
  - q: "Is AI psychosis only a problem for executives, or does it affect regular users too?"
    a: "Every frequent AI user accumulates similar cognitive biases. The mechanism is identical: repeated exposure to confident-sounding outputs trains your brain to skip verification. The only difference is blast radius. A solo developer who blindly trusts AI ships buggy code; a CEO who does the same misallocates millions in budget and steers product direction. Engineers, analysts, writers, and researchers all face the same trap. The mitigation is identical across roles: treat AI output as a draft requiring review, not a finished answer, and build verification into your workflow as a non-negotiable step."
  - q: "What's the practical difference between using AI as a reference signal vs. an arbiter?"
    a: "A reference signal is one input among several that you weigh against your own analysis, domain knowledge, and other sources. An arbiter is the final word that overrides other inputs. The shift happens gradually and quietly. You stop reading the AI's reasoning and only read its conclusion. You stop asking colleagues because the AI already answered. You skip the data check because the AI cited a number. Keep yourself in reference-signal mode by requiring at least one independent verification for any decision above a defined cost threshold."
  - q: "Does this mean I should use AI less for important decisions?"
    a: "No. The fix is not less AI but more structured AI use. For high-stakes decisions, run the same prompt through two different models and compare outputs. Force the AI to argue both sides explicitly before giving a recommendation. Document the AI's stated assumptions and verify each one independently. Set a rule that any AI conclusion driving spend above a threshold requires human sign-off with written reasoning. These guardrails preserve AI's speed advantage while blocking the path from useful tool to unchecked oracle."
  - q: "How does this debate differ from earlier 'AI hype' criticism?"
    a: "Earlier AI hype criticism focused on capability inflation, claiming models do less than marketing suggests. The AI psychosis debate is different in two ways. First, it targets users not vendors, asking how human judgment degrades through repeated AI exposure rather than whether the technology works. Second, it focuses on decision-makers specifically because their cognitive biases scale into organizational outcomes. The shift matters because even if AI capabilities are real and growing, the human side of the loop can still fail, and that failure mode gets worse, not better, as models become more capable."
  - q: "What concrete habits should an AI-heavy workflow include to prevent judgment drift?"
    a: "Build four habits into your routine. First, after any AI-assisted decision, spend two minutes writing what you would do if AI gave the opposite answer. Second, weekly, pick one recent AI output you acted on and verify its key claims from independent sources. Third, keep a log of cases where AI was wrong, and review it before high-stakes decisions to recalibrate trust. Fourth, deliberately seek out human input that might contradict the AI, especially from people whose domain knowledge predates current AI tools. These habits cost minutes and prevent compounding judgment errors."

## 💬 JudyAI Lab Perspective

This podcast episode raises a red flag worth paying attention to: When a CEO's judgment of AI shifts from strategic consideration to near-faith-based reliance, the distortion risk for tech decisions doesn't just stay as a personal bias—it seeps down into the entire organization's judgment framework.

The discussion points to several structural factors: CEOs' decision-making positions naturally create information echo chambers, their heavy dependence on AI for business gains, plus the constant external hype around AI capabilities—all three叠加 make it easy for judgment to go off track. For those of us building and using AI tools in real-world scenarios, this phenomenon reminds us of one thing: The output AI systems give is still fundamentally a statistical result, not a factual arbiter. When decision-makers start equating AI's suggestions with objective truth instead of treating them as reference signals that can be questioned, that's where the blind spot forms. This isn't just an extreme case for execs—anyone who frequently uses AI in their workflow could unknowingly accumulate similar cognitive biases—the difference is just scale and impact scope.

My advice: After using AI for any key decision, spend a few minutes reversely questioning that conclusion: If AI gave you the opposite answer, how would you respond? This habit can effectively reduce blind acceptance of AI output.
