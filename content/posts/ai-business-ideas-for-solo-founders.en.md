> **TL;DR**: AI didn't make solo entrepreneurship easier—it made "one person doing a team's work" possible. Here's 10 real directions my AI team pulled off over the past year. Each one's got people making money from it, but each has its own pitfalls too.

Someone asked me the other day: "You run an AI team, so I want to start too—what should I do?"

I thought about it and realized the question itself is a bit off. It's not "what should I do"—it's "what can I keep doing?"

But I get what he means. AI turned solo entrepreneurship from "impossible" into "some people actually did it," and then you start wondering, so can I?

I ran at it for a year, saw a lot of solo founder case studies, and hit my own bumps along the way. Picking 10 directions that I think really have potential—it's not just a list, it's what I actually see.

## BuiltWith: How One PersonHit $14M/Year

Some of you have heard of BuiltWith—you put in a URL, it tells you what tech that site uses. Gary Brewer built it solo, $14M/year. No funding, no team, no office.

But the point isn't "one person"—it's "one super narrow need executed to the extreme." He didn't build an SEO tool everything bag. He just did "website tech stack detection" one thing, for almost 20 years.

AI era lowered the barrier for this model. You don't need to know all the code, all the architecture. What you need is seeing a gap that nobody's doing well, and filling it with AI.

## 10 Directions Where I Actually See People Making Money

**First, vertical AI SaaS.** Not building a ChatGPT competitor—it's "AI tools for a specific group." Contract review for lawyers, reconciliation for accountants, test paper analysis for tutors. These won't go viral, but each user will pay $50 to $200 a month because it actually saves them time.

**Second, AI workflow outsourcing.** Customers don't want tools—they want results. You use n8n or Zapier to set up an automated workflow, run it for them every month, charge a subscription. I know someone doing e-commerce customer service automation—6 clients, $20K/month revenue.

**Third, content production systems.** Note: "systems" not "content." Anyone can write an article, but being able to consistently output 10 articles a day that match brand tone, have SEO effect, and don't sound AI-written—that's a system. My own team is doing this.

**Fourth, Newsletter paid subscriptions.** Find a vertical,整理 every week: AI news, tools, case studies in that field. Lenny's Newsletter—one person, $1M/year. AI can help you read through 30 info sources, but picking topics and opinions still comes from you.

**Fifth, AI coaching or consulting.** Not teaching people to use AI—using AI to solve the client's problem. Career coaches use AI for personalized resume analysis, fitness coaches use AI for diet tracking, business consultants use AI for market research. Clients pay for "results," not "tools."

**Sixth, AI Agent template sales.** On Gumroad, a well-made Agent prompt template can sell for $49 to $99. My "AI Commander" template moved 10 copies after going live—even though I barely promoted it.

**Seventh, data analysis as a service.** Small businesses have data but nobody knows how to read it. You use AI to generate a weekly report, telling them which product's selling, which customer's leaving, which ad should stop. $299 to $999/month—10x cheaper than hiring an analyst.

**Eighth, vertical community + courses.** This one's tiring but the returns are high. Raven built an AI creator community in Taiwan—17 months from 0 to 10K+ followers, 3K subscribers. The key is "community" not "courses"—people stay for belonging, not just knowledge.

**Ninth, AI-powered local services.** Real estate agents, tutors, pet grooming—most local businesses are still on Excel and LINE. You build an Agent specifically for an industry, handling bookings, quotes, follow-ups. Sell to 10 businesses in one city and you're set.

**Tenth, content translation and localization.** Not the kind like Google Translate—it's "turning English info into something the Chinese-speaking world can actually understand." A well-localized article in a niche can bring consistent traffic. I've seen someone specializing in Japanese AI news localization, funding themselves through Substack subscriptions.

## But There's One Thing AI Can't Help You With

My biggest takeaway from this year: AI made "execution" cheaper, but "judgment" got more expensive.

The bottleneck used to be "can't get it done"—now it's "don't know what to do." AI can help you write 50 articles a day, but when none of them get read, the problem isn't output, it's topic selection. AI can help you run MVPs for 10 products, but when none sell, the problem isn't speed, it's your market sense.

The real advantage for solo founders in the AI era isn't "I do it faster with AI"—it's "I see it more accurately than others."

So of these 10 directions, not a single one can succeed on tools alone. Behind each one, you need real observation of that field, real care, real willingness to grind it out.

## What You Should Do Is Find the Problem You Want to Solve First, Not Learn AI

I've seen too many people get stuck on "I need to learn all the AI tools before I can start."

You can't learn them all. OpenAI dropped something new yesterday, Anthropic's dropping a new model next week, n8n adds hundreds of integrations every month. By the time you learn it all, the market's already gone through several cycles.

The more practical approach is reverse—What problem do you want to solve? Which group of people? What method are they using now? How many times cheaper can AI make that process?

Once you have the answer, the tools you need will surface naturally, and you'll have a direction when you learn them.

My friend didn't pick a direction in the end. He said he wanted to figure out who he wants to help first.
