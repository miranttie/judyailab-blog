  - q: "What does 'hallucination' mean in AI, and why does it matter?"
    a: "A hallucination is when an AI model generates output that sounds confident but is factually wrong or fabricated. It happens because language models predict plausible next tokens, not verified facts. This matters because hallucinated citations, statistics, or code can slip past readers who trust the fluent tone. Always verify AI output against primary sources before publishing, shipping, or making decisions. Treat the model as a fast draft writer, not a fact-checker. For high-stakes work like legal, medical, or financial content, pair the model with retrieval-augmented generation or human review."
  - q: "What is 'training data' and why should non-technical users care?"
    a: "Training data is the massive corpus of text, images, or code an AI model learned from. It defines what the model knows, the biases it inherits, and the cutoff date of its knowledge. Non-technical users should care because the same model can sound brilliant on topics well-covered in its training data and confidently wrong on niche or recent ones. If you ask about events after the cutoff or in low-resource languages, expect weaker answers. Knowing this helps you pick the right tool and avoid trusting outputs in domains the model never properly saw."
  - q: "What's the difference between a 'model', an 'API', and a 'product' like ChatGPT?"
    a: "A model is the underlying neural network, like GPT-5 or Claude Opus 4.7. An API is the programmatic interface developers call to send prompts and receive outputs, billed per token. A product like ChatGPT or Claude.ai is the consumer app wrapping a model with chat UI, memory, file upload, and safety layers. Confusing these leads to bad decisions: paying ChatGPT Plus doesn't unlock API access, and API costs scale with usage while subscriptions are flat. Pick the API for building apps, and the subscription product for personal daily use."
  - q: "How do I quickly learn AI jargon without reading a 50-term glossary?"
    a: "Skip the alphabetical glossary approach. Instead, pick three terms you've seen this week, write your own one-sentence definition, then check it against a reputable source like TechCrunch's glossary or Anthropic's documentation. Repeat weekly. This active recall sticks far better than passive reading. Focus first on the high-frequency core: model, prompt, token, context window, fine-tuning, RAG, agent, and hallucination. These eight cover roughly 80% of AI conversations. Save niche terms like MoE or quantization until you actually need them for a specific decision."
  - q: "Why does AI product copy fail when it leans on technical jargon?"
    a: "Jargon-heavy copy assumes the reader already shares your vocabulary, but most prospective users don't. When someone reads 'leverages a fine-tuned transformer with RAG-augmented inference,' they bounce. Clear copy describes the outcome in their language: 'finds the answer inside your documents in seconds.' Plain language isn't dumbing down, it's removing friction between intent and understanding. Test your copy by reading it to someone outside tech. If they can't repeat back what your product does, rewrite it. Conversion rates on landing pages routinely double when teams strip out unexplained acronyms."
  - q: "Who is an AI glossary actually useful for?"
    a: "AI glossaries help three audiences: newcomers entering the field who need shared vocabulary, journalists and marketers translating technical news for general readers, and product managers bridging engineering and business teams. They are less useful for working ML engineers, who already know the terms and need depth instead. If you're a founder, writer, executive, or curious professional trying to follow AI news without feeling lost, bookmark one reliable glossary like TechCrunch's or Hugging Face's. Use it as a lookup reference, not a textbook to read cover to cover."
  - q: "What's the biggest mistake people make when first learning AI terms?"
    a: "The biggest mistake is memorizing definitions without context. Knowing that 'temperature controls randomness' means nothing until you actually adjust it in an API call and see outputs change. Terms only stick when tied to a concrete use case. The second mistake is faking fluency in meetings by parroting words like 'agentic' or 'multimodal' without grasping the trade-offs. This backfires the moment someone asks a follow-up. Better to ask 'can you explain what you mean by that here?' Curiosity earns respect; pretend-expertise gets exposed fast in technical conversations."
